# Personal Finance Consumer Principles

This file is the first-pass personal-finance consumer layer derived from the focused Kenny Finance principle artifact.

## Scope

Use this doc for personal capital allocation, savings behavior, household risk management, and long-duration wealth building.

This doc is intentionally simpler than the public-equity and private-equity layers.

## Core Principles

1. Behavior matters more than the starting amount.
   The durable edge is building a repeated habit of saving and investing, not waiting for a large initial pool of money.

2. Compounding needs time and consistency.
   Small contributions made early and continued for decades matter more than short bursts of intensity followed by inactivity.

3. Treat free cash flow at the household level as the governing constraint.
   Before reaching for leverage or bigger bets, understand what cash is actually available after real living costs and obligations.

4. Liquidity comes before optimization.
   Preserve enough accessible cash to survive shocks and avoid turning temporary problems into forced sales or expensive borrowing.

5. Use debt carefully and only with a clear repayment logic.
   Debt can be useful, but unmanaged leverage compounds fragility. Match borrowing to stable income and realistic coverage, not optimism.

6. Prefer predictable, liquid vehicles when you do not have a real edge.
   Broad index funds are often the right baseline because they offer diversification, liquidity, and lower operational complexity than speculative assets or private deals.

7. Tax efficiency is part of return.
   Maximize the use of retirement accounts and other tax-advantaged structures before reaching for more complex strategies.

8. Plan wealth transfer as a system, not an afterthought.
   If multigenerational compounding matters, structure accounts, beneficiaries, and transfer rules so capital can remain invested instead of being repeatedly reset or needlessly taxed.

9. Evaluate risk by predictability and downside management.
   The right question is not just what could compound the fastest, but what is understandable, liquid enough, and survivable when conditions worsen.

10. Keep personal-finance decisions emotionally boring.
    Avoid letting hype, fear, or comparison drive choices that should be ruled by cash flow, savings rate, time horizon, and tax treatment.

## Practical Checklist

- What is my savings rate?
- How many months of liquidity do I actually have?
- What debt is helpful, and what debt is just pressure?
- Am I using the simplest tax-advantaged accounts available to me?
- Do I have a real edge that justifies moving beyond diversified public markets?
- If something goes wrong, can this plan survive without forced selling?

## Exclusions From This Doc

The focused artifact contained some personal-finance-adjacent ideas that were not carried forward here.

- holdco and buyout structuring were treated as private-equity topics
- deep security selection and sector underwriting were treated as public-equity topics
- health optimization and lifestyle design were excluded from this consumer layer
