# Private Equity Consumer Principles

This file is the first-pass private-equity consumer layer derived from the focused Kenny Finance principle artifact.

## Scope

Use this doc for buying, financing, diligencing, and operating private businesses.

This is not a generic career guide and it is not primarily about public-stock selection.

## Core Principles

1. Start with cash flow durability.
   The first question is whether the business generates reliable, repeatable cash flow that can survive debt service, operational mistakes, and a weaker market.

2. Underwrite operational reality before spreadsheet elegance.
   A model that ignores labor issues, culture, supply chain friction, integration pain, or subcontractor risk is not a serious buyout model.

3. Perform qualitative risk screening before detailed valuation.
   Check market structure, disruption risk, customer behavior, competitive pressure, and regulatory burden before spending time on precision math.

4. Prefer defensible niches over generic categories.
   Localized, specialized, or regulated businesses often have better pricing power and more resilient demand than broad undifferentiated operators.

5. Deep local diligence beats generic market data.
   Good private-equity sourcing depends on ground truth: local demand, local competitors, actual customer behavior, and local regulatory conditions.

6. Treat licensing and regulation as real barriers to entry.
   In many boring businesses, compliance burden is not just a cost. It is part of the moat and directly affects continuity, margins, and buyer interest.

7. Focus on liquidity, not just profit.
   Lenders care about near-term operational cash flow. A business can look profitable and still fail the practical test of paying obligations on time.

8. Use leverage as a controlled cycle.
   Debt should expand only when stable cash flow supports it. Refinancing, SBA usage, and lender relationships should increase capacity without putting the whole structure at risk.

9. Protect debt capacity.
   The right move is often to earn the next turn of leverage through execution rather than maximize debt on day one.

10. Holdco structure matters when capital allocation skill is real.
    A flexible capital base can be powerful, but only if there is discipline in deciding when to buy, hold, improve, refinance, or walk away.

11. LOIs are access mechanisms, not trophies.
    The point of an LOI is to earn diligence access, improve information quality, and move toward a better decision, not to celebrate progress before the facts are known.

12. Value creation requires triangulated execution.
    Real returns come from combining good sourcing, correct structure, strong operator judgment, and disciplined follow-through after close.

13. Platform and rollup logic only works when operations can actually integrate.
    Multiple expansion is not automatic. It has to be earned through centralized systems, better execution, and real operating improvement.

14. Private equity is not a side quest.
    Serious acquisition work requires maturity, time capacity, and operational depth. It is closer to running a business than casually picking an asset.

## Practical Checklist

- Is cash flow durable enough to support this structure?
- What breaks if revenue softens or labor costs rise?
- What local facts matter more than industry averages?
- Which regulatory or licensing constraints shape the moat?
- What does a 13-week cash view imply about risk?
- Is the debt package expanding future options or reducing them?
- What specific post-close improvements justify the return target?

## Exclusions From This Doc

Some cross-domain artifact items were left out of this consumer layer.

- public-market thesis and ticker-pitch mechanics belong in public equity
- family compounding, index-fund discipline, and tax-advantaged saving belong in personal finance
- community or personality-driven content was excluded unless it clearly improved diligence or sourcing
