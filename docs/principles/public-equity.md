# Public Equity Consumer Principles

This file is the first-pass public-equity consumer layer derived from the focused Kenny Finance principle artifact.

## Scope

Use this doc for principles that apply to buying, underwriting, and monitoring listed businesses.

Do not use this doc as the primary reference for buyout process, SBA leverage, or household budgeting decisions.

## Core Principles

1. Start with the investment thesis.
   A public-equity idea should state what the business is, why it is mispriced, what the moat is, what could break the thesis, and what would have to happen for the position to work.

2. Demand explicit risk, holding period, and performance hurdle.
   A name should earn capital only if it can outperform existing alternatives after accounting for downside, time horizon, and opportunity cost.

3. Anchor analysis in free cash flow, not surface profitability.
   EBITDA, narrative growth, and hype are secondary to the actual cash the business can use for reinvestment, debt service, buybacks, or dividends.

4. Read the balance sheet before trusting the income statement.
   Cash, receivables, inventory, PP&E, and the full debt stack often reveal the real resilience or fragility of the business faster than headline earnings do.

5. Use liquidation value or asset backing as a reality check.
   In stressed or unpopular names, compare market cap to tangible assets and net liabilities to see whether the market is pricing the business below a defensible floor.

6. Screen for moat before valuation precision.
   Cheapness alone is not enough. Prefer businesses with structural advantages such as brand, regulation, switching costs, installed base, captive service revenue, or scarce infrastructure.

7. Prefer infrastructure and picks-and-shovels over pure speculation.
   In fast-moving sectors, the better public-equity bet is often the regulated or indispensable supplier rather than the most volatile end asset.

8. Treat regulation as part of the moat, not a footnote.
   In sectors like crypto custody, healthcare, fintech, or utilities, compliance depth can be the main barrier to entry and a core source of long-term value.

9. Favor recurring, sticky, or service-heavy revenue.
   Businesses with repeat usage, servicing demand, maintenance contracts, or embedded workflows usually deserve more trust than one-time transaction stories.

10. Separate macro tailwind from business quality.
    Commodity moves, dollar weakness, reshoring, AI capex, or sector rotation can help a stock, but they do not replace underwriting the company-specific economics.

11. Require evidence that strategic change is translating into financial results.
    Turnaround stories, platform transformations, and major capex programs deserve skepticism until they show real profitability, returns, or market-share gains.

12. Use mandatory filings and standardized metrics wherever possible.
    Prefer primary documents, consistent credit metrics, and comparable reporting over marketing language, management spin, or informal summaries.

## Practical Checklist

- What is the thesis in one sentence?
- What is the moat?
- What does free cash flow actually look like after real costs?
- What does the balance sheet say about survival?
- Is the current price below a conservative estimate of value?
- What specific risks could permanently impair the thesis?
- Why is this better than an index fund or another current holding?
